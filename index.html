Community-Based Participatory Research Requirements

Foundational Principle

No cultural validation without authentic community partnership and shared research design authority. This isn't just about recruiting diverse participants - it's about ensuring cultural communities have genuine control over how they're studied.

Partnership Development Process

Community Partnership Requirements: 1. Community advisory boards for each cultural context 2. Shared research design and interpretation authority 3. Community benefit requirements (research must help the community) 4. Long-term relationship commitment beyond single studies 5. Cultural knowledge protection protocols 6. Fair compensation and resource sharing agreements

Why These Requirements Matter

Shared authority: Communities have final say over research questions, methods, and how results are interpreted and shared. This prevents extractive research that benefits academics but not communities.

Community benefit: Research must provide direct value to participating communities, not just advance academic careers or institutional interests.

Knowledge protection: Cultural communities retain ownership over their knowledge and can restrict access to sacred, sensitive, or proprietary information.

Long-term commitment: Relationships extend beyond single studies to ongoing collaboration and mutual support.

What Cultural Adaptation Actually Means

Not surface-level changes: This isn't just translating questionnaires into different languages or using different examples. It's understanding how fundamental concepts like "identity," "time," and "coordination" are understood differently across cultures.

Functional equivalence across cultures: The framework proposes that different cultures achieve equivalent coordination outcomes through different but equally valid approaches. Adaptation involves mapping these equivalences.

Example - Temporal Coherence:

  • Western: "Can you connect your past self to your current self?"
  • Buddhist: "Can you maintain mindful awareness while accepting the impermanent nature of experience?"
  • Indigenous: "Can you honor your ancestors while adapting to current circumstances for future generations?"
  • Confucian: "Can you fulfill your current roles while developing according to proper cultivation?"

All four approaches address temporal coherence, but through culturally authentic frameworks.

1. Cross-Cultural Gaming Studies Protocol

Primary Hypothesis

Capability-based coalition formation enhances coordination effectiveness in culturally diverse teams compared to traditional team formation methods.

Study Design Overview

Design: Randomized controlled trial with between-subjects design Target N: 300 participants across 6 cultural groups (50 per group) Cultural Groups: WEIRD, Traditional (East Asian), Indigenous (North American), Religious (Multi-faith), Collectivist, Mixed groups Duration: 3 hours with breaks per gaming session Team Size: 4-6 participants per coalition

What We're Actually Testing

The key question: Do teams formed using capability-based principles coordinate better than teams formed traditionally?

Why gaming: Games provide controlled environments where we can measure coordination without real-world confounding variables. Teams must cooperate to succeed, but we can track exactly how they coordinate.

Cultural diversity focus: We specifically want to test whether diversity helps or hurts when teams use capability-based coordination principles.

Pre-Gaming Assessment Protocol (90 minutes)

Individual PRF Mapping: - Belief Structure Assessment (WAS-47): 30 minutes - Decision-Making Scenario Battery: 25 minutes - Ontological Commitment Survey: 20 minutes - Authenticity Criteria Assessment: 15 minutes Cultural Profile Assessment: - Cultural Background Questionnaire: 15 minutes - Coordination Style Inventory: 10 minutes - Conflict Resolution Style Assessment: 10 minutes - Trust Building Mechanism Survey: 10 minutes

What Each Assessment Measures

Belief Structure Assessment (WAS-47): How certain are you about different claims? How do your beliefs connect to each other?

Decision-Making Scenarios: When faced with moral dilemmas or practical choices, what decision process do you use? What factors matter most?

Ontological Commitment Survey: How do you think about fundamental categories like personhood, causation, time? These shape how you approach coordination.

Authenticity Criteria: What makes you feel like you're being true to yourself? What would make you feel like you're betraying your identity?

Cultural Profile: What coordination approaches did you learn from your cultural background? How do you typically build trust, resolve conflicts, make group decisions?

Real-Time Data Collection During Gaming

Coordination Effectiveness Measures: - Task completion quality (0-100 scale) - Resource sharing efficiency (time-to-share, fairness metrics) - Conflict resolution speed (time from disagreement to resolution) - Communication effectiveness (clarity, responsiveness, cultural accommodation) Physiological Monitoring: - Heart rate variability (stress response indicators) - EEG gamma synchrony (coordination states, requires specialized equipment) - Salivary cortisol (stress hormones, collected at T+0, +2h, +4h) Behavioral Observation: - Turn-taking patterns (who speaks when, interruption frequency) - Cultural accommodation behaviors (adapting style for others) - Authenticity maintenance indicators (staying true to cultural approach)

How We Know Coordination is Actually Happening

Task completion quality: Did they actually solve the problems correctly? Just getting along isn't enough - they need to be effective.

Resource sharing: Are they willing to give up individual advantages for group success? This tests genuine cooperation vs. just politeness.

Conflict resolution: When disagreements arise (they always do), can they work through them constructively rather than avoiding or escalating?

Physiological measures: Body responses show real stress vs. genuine comfort. EEG gamma synchrony indicates when brains are literally "in sync" during coordination.

Cultural accommodation: Are people adapting their communication style to work better with others while still staying authentic to themselves?

Statistical Analysis Plan

Primary Analysis: - ANCOVA: Post-intervention effectiveness controlling for baseline PRF scores - Effect Size: Cohen's d calculation with 95% confidence intervals - Non-inferiority testing: Coordination ‚â• 90% of baseline individual performance Secondary Analyses: - Mediation analysis: ATCF changes mediating condition ‚Üí effectiveness - Network analysis: Communication pattern changes - Qualitative thematic analysis: Coordination experience interviews Power Analysis: - Expected effect size: d = 0.5 (medium effect) - Power: 0.80, Alpha: 0.05 - Required N = 256 (allowing 15% attrition: Target N = 300)

What This Statistical Plan Means

ANCOVA: We compare final coordination scores while accounting for how people started. This ensures any differences come from the intervention, not pre-existing differences.

Effect size d = 0.5: This represents a "medium" improvement - meaningful but not unrealistically large. It's equivalent to the average treated person doing better than 69% of untreated people.

Power = 0.80: 80% chance of detecting a real effect if it exists. This is the standard threshold for psychological research.

Mediation analysis: Tests whether ATCF (temporal coherence) is the mechanism explaining why the intervention works.

Core Framework Principles

1. Bootstrap Resolution of Normative Authority

Normative requirements emerge from the performative necessities of temporal agency itself. Any agent capable of asking "why should I be ethical?" already demonstrates commitment to the temporal coherence requirements that ground ethical coordination.

2. Capability-Based Coordination

Agents coordinate through functional equivalence rather than content agreement. Different beliefs, values, and cognitive architectures can achieve coordination through equivalent capabilities for temporal coherence and responsive interaction.

3. Universal Coordination Principles

Three principles operate across biological, phenomenological, and technological scales:

  • Capability-Based Propositions: Specify abilities rather than content
  • Temporal Coherence Functions: Maintain identity while enabling adaptation
  • Cross-Scale Markup Organization: Enable coordination across implementation substrates

What This Means

A Personal Reality Framework is like a complete "operating system" for how an agent makes sense of the world and coordinates with others. Think of it as containing everything needed to understand how someone thinks, decides, and acts.

How to Read the Notation

  • PRF(A,t): The complete framework for agent A at time t

  • XSC(t): Cross-scale coordination patterns
  • FOP(t): Future-oriented projections

Why This Matters

Instead of treating agents as simple preference-holders or belief-systems, this captures the full complexity of how real people organize their mental lives while still being mathematically precise enough for coordination analysis.

Plain English Translation

What beliefs actually are: Rather than just "I believe X," this captures how confident someone is about different claims and how those beliefs fit together.

  • D (Domain): All the different things the agent has opinions about
  • P (Probability): How likely they think each thing is (0 = impossible, 1 = certain)
  • C (Confidence): How sure they are about their probability estimate

Example: "I think it will rain tomorrow" might be P=0.7 (70% chance) with C=0.8 (pretty confident in that estimate), while "Aliens exist somewhere" might be P=0.6 but C=0.3 (not very confident in that estimate).

The constraint: If you think something is 70% likely, you must think it's 30% unlikely. This prevents logical contradictions.

Understanding the Unified Experience Vector

What this captures: At any moment, you have thoughts, feelings, motivations, and readiness for action. The UEV measures how well these fit together.

Global Feeling Tone (GFT) - Your overall internal state:

  • Integration: Do your different mental processes work together or fight each other?
  • Valence: Overall positive (+1) to negative (-1) feeling
  • Arousal: How activated/energized you are (0 = calm, 1 = highly energized)

Example states:
